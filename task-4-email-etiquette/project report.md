A significant real-world example of a digital communication failure occurred during the 2018 Hawaii missile alert. An emergency management employee accidentally sent a live "Ballistic Missile Threat" alert to the entire state instead of a "test" message because the dropdown menu in the software was poorly labeled and lacked clear visual distinction between testing and live environments. This caused widespread panic for 38 minutes before a correction was issued.

To prevent this, the communication system should have implemented a "Two-Factor Confirmation" process for high-stakes alerts, requiring a second person to authorize the transmission. Additionally, the digital interface should have used clearer UI/UX design, such as color-coding "Test" actions in green and "Live" actions in red. For students like myself at VIT Bhopal, this serves as a reminder to always double-check "Reply All" settings and subject lines, ensuring our digital tools assist rather than hinder our professional communication.
